Research

Interests

My scientific interests are generally within the field of galaxy formation and evolution. In particular, I use cosmic explosions such as gamma-ray bursts (GRBs) and quasars as tools to study the dust, gas and metal content of intervening or host galaxy absorption systems. This allows us to map the chemical enrichment of galaxies through cosmic time. I am also actively involved in the search for and study of the electromagnetic counterparts associated with gravitational wave events, such as merging neutron stars and/or black holes. Finally, I am also currently working on fast radio bursts (FRBs), trying to disentangle the cosmic web in the intergalactic medium and constrain their progenitors through population studies of their host galaxies. See below for a list of the collaborations I am actively involved in and the Publications page for an overview of the published papers I have contributed to.


Collaborations

Stargate (PI: Nial R. Tanvir) is the continuation of the XS-GRB collaboration (sample paper published here), now searching for the most exotic GRBs. We have access to almost all instruments mounted at the four VLT unit telescopes, including several other instruments mounted at smaller telescopes all over the world (such as the NOT). The main science goals of this programme is to investigate and obtain follow-up servations of high-redshift GRBs, short GRBs and otherwise peculiar transients.

ENGRAVE (PI: Stephen Smartt) is a newly founded collaboration, performing follow-up observations of electromagnetic counterparts of gravitational wave sources at the Very Large Telescope detected by LIGO/Virgo (see webpage here).

Fast and fortunate for FRB follow-up (PI: J. Xavier Prochaska) is dedicated to observe and characterize the optical counterparts and host galaxies of fast radio bursts (FRBs) in addition to use FRBs as probes of the cosmic web. An overview of our main science goals and published can be found at this webpage.

ePESSTO+ (PI: Cosimo Inserra) is the extended Public ESO Spectroscopic Survey of Transient Objects” using the New Technology Telescope at the ESO La Silla observatory (see webpage here).
